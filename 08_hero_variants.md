# Hero Variants — Base44 Superagents

*Cold traffic primary. Goal: make a stranger understand and want this in 5 seconds.*
*Built using: copywriting/copy-frameworks, marketing-psychology, copy-editing skills.*

---

## Cold Traffic Context

The person landing here knows:
- AI tools exist and are hyped
- They have recurring work they'd love to offload
- Setting up AI agents sounds complicated

They don't know:
- What a Superagent is specifically
- That Base44 exists or what it does
- Why this is different from ChatGPT or Zapier

The hero must answer in this order:
1. **What is it** — fast, concrete, not abstract
2. **What does it do for me** — specific outcome, not features
3. **Why is it different** — one clear contrast
4. **Low friction entry** — CTA that feels like a small step

---

## The Four Variants

---

### VARIANT A — Outcome-focused
*Formula: "{Achieve desirable outcome} without {pain point}"*
*Angle: Lead with the dream state. Make the contrast implicit.*

**Eyebrow:** Base44 Superagents

**H1:**
> An AI agent that handles your work.
> Without the setup.

**Sub:**
> Tell it what to do — follow up on leads, handle your inbox, send your weekly report, prep your meetings. It runs 24/7 and delivers updates to WhatsApp or Slack. Nothing to install. Nothing to maintain. Just describe what you want and it starts.

**CTA:** Get your Superagent

**Trust nudge:** Takes under a minute · No credit card required

---

**Why this works:**
- H1 splits into two lines intentionally — first line states the promise, second line removes the objection before it forms.
- "Without the setup" does the job of an entire competitor comparison in four words. Cold traffic doesn't need to know about OpenClaw — they just need to know *this* is easier.
- Sub leads with use cases first (concrete), then explains the delivery mechanism (WhatsApp/Slack), then removes friction (nothing to install). That's the right order for cold traffic.
- Weakness: "handles your work" is slightly broad. Works if the visual (chat UI) grounds it in something specific.

---

### VARIANT B — Problem-focused
*Formula: "Stop [pain]. Start [pleasure]."*
*Angle: Open with the frustration. It creates immediate recognition.*

**Eyebrow:** Base44 Superagents

**H1:**
> Stop doing the same things
> every single day.

**Sub:**
> Your AI agent handles the recurring work — inbox triage, lead follow-ups, weekly reports, meeting prep — so you don't have to. Describe what you want in plain language. It runs 24/7 on Base44's infrastructure, connects to your tools in one click, and updates you on WhatsApp. No setup. No servers. No tech skills required.

**CTA:** Get your Superagent

**Trust nudge:** Takes under a minute · No credit card required

---

**Why this works:**
- Opens with a frustration that almost every professional has felt, without naming a specific task. Broader catch.
- "Stop doing the same things every single day" is close to how people describe their actual pain — not "I need an AI agent," but "I'm tired of doing this again."
- Weakness: less differentiated from generic productivity tools. Needs the sub to do more work to make "AI agent" land clearly.

---

### VARIANT C — Specific & Concrete
*Formula: Use-case-first. Show before you tell.*
*Angle: Skip the abstraction entirely. Start with a recognizable scenario.*

**Eyebrow:** Base44 Superagents

**H1:**
> What if your inbox was handled
> before you woke up?

**Sub:**
> That's what a Superagent does. Tell it what to handle — your inbox, your leads, your reports, your calendar. It connects to Gmail, Slack, and WhatsApp in one click and runs 24/7 on Base44's infrastructure. No installation. No servers. Describe what you want and it starts immediately.

**CTA:** Get your Superagent

**Trust nudge:** Takes under a minute · No credit card required

---

**Why this works:**
- Opens with a single, visceral scenario that most professionals have fantasized about. Not abstract — extremely concrete.
- The question format (Zeigarnik effect) leaves an open loop the reader's brain wants to close. The answer is in the sub.
- "That's what a Superagent does" is the cleanest possible category definition for cold traffic — it anchors the abstract term to something they just imagined.
- Weakness: inbox is only one use case. People who don't care about inbox triage might bounce before the sub. Mitigated by the sub listing other use cases immediately.
- Best paired with a visual showing the inbox chat script specifically.

---

### VARIANT D — Differentiation-focused
*Formula: "The [category] that [key differentiator]"*
*Angle: For slightly warmer traffic — people who've heard of AI agents but haven't found one that works.*

**Eyebrow:** Base44 Superagents

**H1:**
> The AI agent that's already running.

**Sub:**
> Most AI agents require hours of setup, a server you maintain, and technical skills most people don't have. Superagents don't. Describe what you want in plain language — it connects to your tools, runs 24/7 on Base44's infrastructure, and sends updates to WhatsApp or Slack. Ready in under a minute.

**CTA:** Get your Superagent

**Trust nudge:** Takes under a minute · No credit card required

---

**Why this works:**
- "Already running" is emotionally powerful — it implies the hard work is done, you just have to show up.
- Sub opens by naming the contrast ("most AI agents require…") which works for anyone who's ever googled "AI agent" and been intimidated.
- Weakness: relies on the reader knowing what "AI agents" are. Works for warmer traffic (people who've explored the category); may confuse true cold traffic who haven't.

---

## Recommendation

**Lead with Variant C for cold traffic.**

Here's why:

1. **It starts with a scenario, not a category.** Cold traffic doesn't know what a "Superagent" or even an "AI agent" means yet. Opening with "what if your inbox was handled before you woke up?" gives them something to feel before they have to understand a product category.

2. **The sub does the definition work.** "That's what a Superagent does" anchors the term to the scenario they just imagined. Clean, earned, not forced.

3. **It's the most scroll-stopping.** On paid social especially, the question format creates an open loop that compels reading. On the page, it creates immediate personal relevance.

4. **The weakness is manageable.** Inbox is the most universally relatable use case — almost everyone with a professional life has felt inbox overload. And the sub immediately lists other use cases for people who don't care about email.

**A/B test: Variant C vs. Variant A.**
Variant A ("without the setup") is the safest, broadest choice. Variant C is more specific but likely higher-converting for the right audience. Test both.

## Visual Recommendation (Hero Section)

The visual must show a completed action, not a setup screen.

**Best option:** Animated chat UI showing one full arc:
1. User types: *"Scan my inbox overnight. Flag urgent stuff, draft replies to the easy ones, and send me a WhatsApp summary at 7am."*
2. Agent responds: *"Done. First summary tomorrow at 7am ☀️"*
3. Cut to: WhatsApp notification arriving the next morning

This mirrors the H1 scenario exactly — creates a tight headline ↔ visual pairing that removes any ambiguity about what the product actually does.

**Why animation over static:** Static screenshots show a state. Animation shows a *story* — which is what converts cold traffic. They don't need to read everything. They see it working.

## What NOT to put in the hero (for cold traffic)

- ~~Integration logos~~ — save for the social proof bar directly below. Hero is not the place.
- ~~Pricing hints~~ — too early. They don't believe the value yet.
- ~~"Powered by Base44"~~ — cold traffic doesn't know Base44. It adds nothing yet. Let the product speak first.
- ~~"No credit card required" as the main CTA nudge~~ — include it, but secondary. The main nudge should be speed ("under a minute") since setup anxiety is the primary barrier.
- ~~Technical capabilities list~~ — "connects to 100+ tools", "supports MCP", etc. That's for the benefits section, not the hero.
